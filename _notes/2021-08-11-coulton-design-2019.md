Coulton and colleagues point at the limitation of [[human-centred-design-hcd]] in the light of the Internet of Things.

They argue that HCD aims to reduce complexity, which many interpret as increasing simplicity and perhaps a limitation of the view of Design itself. However, with IoT, maximising simplicity often disenfranchises and de-privileges the user. Data brings many aspects that have no relevance nor impact on the user's activity. As an example, the authors use connected TVs, which collect and sell personal data. In this context, the interaction (i.e. watching TV) does not represent the data generated and its use. Thus, obscuring the role of data diminishes user agency. Simplicity can also bring security and privacy issues, a paradox to HCD.

Coulton and colleagues review the field of HCI, expanding HCD beyond the anthropocentric perspective. There is an increasing complexity emerging out of the interdependent cohabitation of humans with things. In addition, there is no 'centre' such as a human anymore. Placing HCD as a prevalent design philosophy, Coulton and colleagues stress the need for design tools that reflect the nature of these new ecologies coming out of the IoT 'networkification'.

They explore the metaphor of a constellation to describe the IoT, devices as stars that can be looked at individually or as a group. Thus, one can reflect on different interpretations depending on cultures and backgrounds.
