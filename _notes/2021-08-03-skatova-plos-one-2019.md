In this paper, the authors use the term data donation, referring to request data from commercial data controllers and donating it for research benefiting the public good. In this context, data portability is a critical enabler of data donation. Data subjects can request their data from all data controllers and donate them. However, legally data controllers have up to a month to answer personal data requests.

The core question is: would people donate their data from commercial data controllers for academic health research? What would motivate them to engage in this prosocial act?

The authors conduct a large-scale survey to uncover the intentions and reasons to donate personal data with three purposes from the literature:
- opportunity to achieve self-benefits
- social duty (the strongest predictor of data donation)
- need to understand the purpose of data donation.

They highlight that self-serving motives have negative associations with intentions to donate.
