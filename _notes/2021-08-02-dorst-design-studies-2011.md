In this paper, Dorst teases out what Design Thinking is through formal logic. He aims at formalising Design Thinking for other fields and particularly business and management of organisations.

Design thinking helps to deal with complex, open-ended problems. Starting from the equation `What (thing)` + `How (working principle)` leads to `Result (observed)`, he describes:
- With __Deduction__ we know `what` and `how`, we deduct the `result`.
- With __Induction__ we know `what` and the `result`, we induce the `how`. Induction is a typical pattern for Science as we propose a working principle (forming a hypothesis) to informs discoveries.

In design, the focus is on creating values to be attained rather than facts as a result. Thus, it twists the equation towards `What` + `How` leads to `Value (aspired)`. 

Dorst continues with Abduction, the reasoning pattern of productive thinking, coming in 2 forms:

- Abduction-1, we miss the `what`. This is often the designer's case to propose a 'thing' that operate with a working principle (closed problem solving).
- Abduction-2, we only know the value we want to achieve. This requires design reasoning.

At this stage, Dorst introduces the frame as the connecting point between `working principle` and `value`.

>IF we look at the problem situation from this viewpoint, and adopt the working principle associated with that position, THEN we will create the value we are striving for.

Thus, abduction-2 occurs by creating a frame (`working principle` + `targeted values`), then applying abduction-1 to uncover the `what`. Finally, we test the completed equation (designing and testing a thing).

There are design practices of three categories to go through the Abduction-2 process effectively:

- kinds of design activities 'Formulating', 'Representing', 'Moving', 'Evaluating' and 'Managing'.
- level of design expertise: 'Naïve', 'Novice', 'Advanced Beginner', 'Competent', 'Expert', 'Master' and 'Visionary'.
- layers of design practice: project, process, field (organisational, intellectual and physical environment).

Dorst highlights that the `field` is the repertoire of frames that the design firms regularly work with. Designers design their design process, a process their shape through their experience in designing in a given field. Vision in Product (ViP) by Hekkert and Dijk is one strategy for creating frames.

In practice, designers describe 'getting close' to the situation, gathering 'first-hand experience' and capturing the problem's richness'. Dorst argues that this is about gathering clues for the emergence of themes, which inform the development of new frames.
