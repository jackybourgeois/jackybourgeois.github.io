**Open for application!**

Sexuality is influenced by several factors including social norms, gender roles, educational background, and religious beliefs. These factors, throughout history, have influenced, and often hindered, women's attitudes towards sex, pleasure, and even their own bodies [4,7]. In the 1920s as vibrators were introduced as a tool to induce pleasure, as opposed to a "cure" to hysteria, social norms around female sexuality started to change and masturbation began to be seen as a common (and somewhat acceptable) practice [6,7]. Vibrators have facilitated the normalization of female masturbation and have played a role in empowering women and allowing them to experience a positive and pleasurable sexuality [3,4,5]. Furthermore, the use of vibrators has proven to improve women’s self-awareness, body image, self-esteem, intimate health, and overall sexual pleasure. However, women’s experiences with masturbation, vibrators, and other sex toys, are often complicated. 

Today, the sex toy industry for (empowering) women has grown significantly, and through technological development, it is in the process of becoming smart [2]. Companies like Lovense and Lioness are developing smart connected vibrators equipped with sensors that continuously collect intimate data, with the potential to allow us to explore female masturbation from a different perspective and bring visibility to women's sexual practices and preferences. However, being highly intimate, they introduce unknowns regarding how they should be handled and the privacy norms that should govern them.

The aim of this project is twofold. First, we aim to create a deep understanding of how women’s sexual experience is empowered and supported by masturbation. Through a combination of sensor data from smart connected vibrators, obtained through data donation, with women’s own narratives and contextual insights. Second, we aim to understand women’s privacy norms around sex data. 

# Your Role

In this project you will embrace the fuzzy front end of the design process. By integrating quantitative data from connected products and services in the wild (e.g., menstrual tracking technologies, smart connected sex toys) with contextual insights from observations and interviews to explore the design space of near-future intimate technologies that empower and support women. 

You are a curious student, critical of the role that technology plays in people's daily lives and experiences. You are interested in designing with data and exploring how different data sources can be integrated to create deep insights at scale. You feel comfortable manipulating sensor data, creating data visualizations and using methods such as participatory data analysis, observations, and interviews. 

# Outcome and Evaluation

You are expected to integrate qualitative and quantitative data to produce rich and meaningful contextual insights that can be transformed into design considerations informing the design space of intimate technologies.
